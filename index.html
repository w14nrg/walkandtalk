About MENd — Why I started this

MENd is a Community Interest Company offering free, confidential, one-on-one walk and talk sessions in local parks. I started MENd because personal, peer conversations saved my life — and I know many other men need the same: an informal, caring space to say what they can’t say elsewhere. Sessions are 45–60 minutes and intentionally simple: a walk, a safe listener, and a coffee if you want.

Men's mental health: the facts & why walking helps

  • Suicide rates remain high

    In England & Wales the combined male suicide rate was around 17.6 deaths per 100,000 (2024 registrations) — substantially higher than the female rate and highlighting why targeted, low-barrier services for men are vital. [source]

  • Prevalence of common mental disorders

    Around one in eight men (roughly 12–15%) experience a common mental health condition in a given week, and rates have been rising over recent years, especially among young adults. Early, informal help can stop problems escalating. [source]

  • Men are less likely to access talking therapies

    Only about 36% of referrals to NHS talking therapies are for men — which suggests many men face barriers to accessing formal therapy. Peer-led walk-and-talks offer a lower-barrier, familiar entry-point to open up. [source]

Why walking in parks helps — short, evidence-backed points

  • Nature + movement = measurable mental health gains. Outdoor activity in natural spaces improves mood and reduces anxiety and depressive symptoms more than equivalent urban indoor exercise. [source]
  • Walking reduces depressive symptoms. Recent studies and meta-analyses show walking (regular moderate activity or meeting simple step goals) is associated with significant reductions in depression and anxiety symptoms. Even modest, regular walks are beneficial. [source]
  • Peer, non-clinical contact lowers stigma. Many men are more comfortable opening up while walking side-by-side rather than face-to-face; that practical difference increases willingness to share and keeps people engaged with support. [source]

MENd provides a practical, low-pressure option to start talking — which can be the difference between isolation and getting support early.
